So what happened? The equivalence panels, Q, require that any two judges of the same nationality on the same panel, be in the same block, if they are not already. This means that the total variation of the empirical distribution form the symmetric estimate of the panel is bounded by something smaller than 1.When there are multiple judges from the same country, it cannot be completely antisymmetric (even if the panel is totally ordered and the judges with the same nationalities are next to each other) because we may apply a permutation transposing two slots where judges from the same country are without a sign change. This also makes sense with various panel Orig distributions: if there are more judges that are members of a country with multiplicity, the more symmetric the panel must be. Using the picture above and to the right of this paragraph, the orig-frequencies of the βs with mean < 0.2 are (3,2) and (2,2,1), for βs with 0.2 < mean < 0.5 orig-frequencies are (2,2,1),(3,1,1),(4,1), for βs with 0.5 < mean < 0.8 orig-frequencies are (2,1,1,1), (3,1,1), and for βs with mean > 0.8  orig-frequencies are (1,1,1,1,1),(2,1,1,1). The above are not disjoint because β corresponds to panel compositions which gives multiple βs with the same frequencies. I can re-run everything using orig-frequency to determine a partition of the sample. This probably explains why the partial sums and cumulative sums for Lns vs. Qns look the way they do.
